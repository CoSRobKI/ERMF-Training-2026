Module 2: Recognising and Reporting Incidents

Something has just gone wrong — or you suspect it has. What you do in the next 24 hours determines whether it gets resolved or becomes a regulatory problem. This module covers how to recognise an Incident, what to do immediately, and how the reporting clock works.

All staff ~10 minutes CPS 220 / 230 / 234

  • How regulatory notification works — and why your speed matters for APRA's deadlines.

When you see something — the four steps
1
Assume it is an Incident. Even if you're unsure. Even if "nothing happened." The framework will sort the rest.
2
Escalate immediately. To your manager, or directly to Risk & Compliance if your manager is unreachable.
3
Log it in Drova within required policy timeframes. If you don't know how to log in Drova, escalate and ask — don't delay reporting because of the system.
4
Let the framework decide the rest. Classification, severity rating, regulatory reportability — all of that is Risk & Compliance's job. Your job is to raise it.

Section 1 of 4 — What is an Incident?
1
Defining an Incident
An Incident is any unplanned event that disrupts, or has the potential to disrupt, our operations, compromises information security, or breaches a regulatory or contractual obligation. It includes near-misses — events that could have caused harm but didn't, by luck or quick action. A near-miss is still an Incident.
An Incident does not have to result in financial loss or a confirmed compliance breach to count. If something unplanned has happened that touches our processes, people, systems, or obligations — it is an Incident.
The three Incident Types
Information Security IncidentAny event that compromises (or attempts to compromise) the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of our information. Includes data breaches, malware or ransomware, unauthorised access, phishing.
Operational Risk IncidentAny failure in people, processes, or systems that disrupts a critical operation — or events arising from outside the business. Includes internal and external fraud and any unintentional financial loss or gain.
Compliance IncidentAn act or omission that breaches — or might breach — a legal, regulatory, or internal policy requirement. Includes APRA standards, AUSTRAC AML/CTF obligations, privacy laws.
Don't worry about getting the category right on first sight. Raise it, and Line 2 will help classify. The mistake to avoid is not raising it because you weren't sure which box it belonged in.
The 24-hour expectation
All Incidents must be logged in Drova promptly and within required policy timeframes — typically within 24 hours of discovery. Escalation to your manager (or directly to Risk & Compliance if your manager is unavailable) must happen as soon as practicable. This is not a "next week" item.
Your role vs the framework's role
A common reason staff hesitate is uncertainty — "what if I get the category wrong, the severity wrong, or escalate something that turns out to be nothing?" That hesitation is the risk. Here is the division of work:
Your role — Line 1

If you stay in your column, you cannot get this wrong. The biggest single failure mode in Incident management is staff trying to do the framework's job for it — assessing, classifying, deciding it's not serious enough — instead of raising it.
Do not delay escalation or logging while deciding whether something meets the definition of an Incident. If in doubt, raise it.

Section 2 of 4 — Spotting an Incident
2
Scenario: the near-miss
Operations — Wednesday morning
A bulk payment file was loaded into the system overnight with an extra zero on one line — a member who should have received $1,200 was queued for $12,000. The error was caught by a colleague during the morning review check, before the payment ran. The file was corrected and the payment went out at the right amount. "No harm done," says the colleague. "The control worked."
Is this an Incident?
Select the best response.
Near-misses matter — sometimes more than incidents that cause harm
  • A near-miss is still an unplanned event with the potential to disrupt operations. That's the definition of an Incident.
  • The detective control worked this time. But why did the error reach the file? That root cause is the real risk.
  • If we only log Incidents that cause harm, we never learn from the ones that nearly did.
  • Logging a near-miss is not about blame — it's about understanding the upstream cause before it happens again with worse luck.
  • If an Incident is rated Medium or higher, a Root Cause Analysis is mandatory, and remediation must be tracked as an Issue through the Issue Management Framework — covered in Module 3.

Section 2 of 4 — Knowing what type
2
Scenario: the misdirected email
Distribution — Friday afternoon
A colleague sends a list of 30 members' policy details to the wrong external email address — an adviser at a completely different firm. They realise within minutes and call the recipient, who confirms they have deleted the email and not opened the attachment. Your colleague says: "It's fine, they deleted it. No need to make a big deal of it."
How should this be classified — and what should happen?
Why this is serious
Personal information about 30 members was sent to someone unauthorised. Whether or not they read it, the data left our control. That triggers an assessment under the Privacy Act (the OAIC requires assessment within 30 days for an eligible data breach), and potentially under APRA CPS 234 if it points to a material information security control weakness. A verbal assurance from the recipient is not evidence — and it is not the staff member's call to decide whether it needs to be reported. The framework decides. Your job is to raise it.

Section 3 of 4 — The first hour
3
Scenario: the system outage
Member Services — Tuesday, 10:15am
The system used to record member transactions has gone down. Members are calling and you cannot access their records. A colleague is suggesting everyone "just write it down on paper and we'll catch up when it comes back." Another is saying "Let's wait an hour — it'll probably come back on its own."
What is the most important thing to do right now?
The first hour — what good looks like
  • Contain first. Stop the immediate harm if you safely can — disconnect, lock, pause, escalate.
  • Tell your manager. Within minutes, not hours. If your manager is unreachable, go directly to Risk & Compliance.
  • Preserve evidence. Don't delete emails, files, or screenshots. The investigation needs them.
  • Log it in Drova promptly and within required policy timeframes (typically within 24 hours of discovery). A formal record starts the framework, the clock, and the regulatory assessment.
  • Don't decide if it's reportable. That decision is made through KeyInvest's Risk & Compliance governance process, in line with the Incident Management Policy. Your job is to surface it.
Critical Operations and CPS 230
Under APRA CPS 230, a disruption to a critical operation — for example, our ability to record member transactions or pay benefits — may require notification to APRA within 24 hours if tolerances are exceeded. That assessment cannot start until the Incident is escalated. Fast internal reporting is what protects KeyInvest's ability to meet that timeframe.

Scenario: the weekend discovery
Any team member — Saturday morning
You're checking your work email from home over the weekend and notice something concerning — a system access log showing unauthorised attempts to access member records overnight on Friday. You're not sure if anything was actually compromised. Your manager is on annual leave. A colleague suggests waiting until Monday "because Risk & Compliance aren't in on the weekend anyway."
What do you do?
Key regulatory notification deadlines
24 hrs
APRA — CPS 230. Disruption to a critical operation outside tolerance.
24 hrs
AUSTRAC — SMR. Suspicious matter report where CTF-related (3 business days otherwise).
72 hrs
APRA — CPS 234. Material information security Incident.
10 days
APRA — CPS 220. Significant breach of, or material deviation from, the Risk Management Framework.
30 days
ASIC. Significant breach of a core obligation under the Corporations Act.
30 days
OAIC. Eligible data breach assessment must be completed; notification as soon as practicable thereafter.
Every one of these clocks starts at discovery, not at the start of business hours. Your fast escalation is what protects KeyInvest's ability to meet these deadlines. Risk & Compliance and the CRO have on-call processes for serious matters — use them.

Your key takeaways
  • An Incident is any unplanned event that disrupts — or could have disrupted — operations, security, or compliance. Near-misses count.
  • Three Incident Types: Information Security, Operational Risk, Compliance. Don't worry about getting it right — raise it, Line 2 will classify.
  • Log every Incident in Drova promptly and within required policy timeframes (typically within 24 hours of discovery). Escalate to your manager (or directly to Risk & Compliance) as soon as practicable.
  • First hour: contain, tell your manager, preserve evidence, log it. Don't try to investigate alone, and don't decide reportability yourself.
  • Regulatory notification clocks start at discovery — not at the start of business hours. Escalate on weekends if you have to. Risk & Compliance will determine the applicable obligations.
  • Incidents often lead to Issues. Where remediation is required, the action is tracked through the Issue Management Framework — covered in Module 3.
