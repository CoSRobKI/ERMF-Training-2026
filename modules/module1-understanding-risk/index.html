Section 1 of 4 — The foundation
1
Why we manage risk
KeyInvest exists to serve and protect Customers and Members' Interests. In practice that means investing Member funds to deliver suitable returns — without exposing them to undue risk. The same trade-off applies to everything else we do: invest enough in our processes, people, and controls to keep risk within appetite, but never so much that we erode the financial strength we exist to protect.
The three material risk categories
Strategic RiskFailure to set or execute strategies that align with our purpose and serve the long-term interests of Customers and Members. Includes Members' Interest & conduct, capability & culture, capital sufficiency, strategic performance, the external environment, and strategic partnerships.
Financial RiskFailure to manage investment, credit, or liquidity exposures in a way that protects our capital strength and our ability to meet financial obligations.
Operational RiskFailure or disruption of internal processes, systems, or people that impacts our ability to deliver services to Customers and Members, or to meet regulatory obligations. Includes business operations, technology, and regulation & legal.
Strategic and Operational risks together are called non-financial risks. Most of the risks you will see day to day are operational.
Risk appetite — the boundary
The Board approves a Risk Appetite Statement (RAS) every year. It defines, in clear terms, the level and type of risk KeyInvest is prepared to take on behalf of Customers and Members. Every material decision we make must sit within that appetite. If a decision pushes us outside appetite, it must be escalated — not absorbed quietly.

Section 2 of 4 — Risk appetite in practice
2
Scenario: the helpful shortcut
Operations team — month-end
Your team is running behind on month-end processing. A team leader suggests skipping the second-reviewer check on a batch of low-value transactions "just this once" to catch up. They say: "They're all under $5,000, no one's going to lose sleep over it. We can document it after the fact."
What should you do?
Select the best response.
Why this matters
  • A control bypassed "just this once" is a control that was not in place — regardless of the dollar value.
  • Operating outside appetite requires escalation and approval through the proper channels — it is not something a team leader can authorise informally.
  • Documenting after the fact does not restore the control. The risk has already crystallised.
  • If a process is creating pressure to skip controls, that itself is a risk worth raising.
  • Speaking up is not disloyalty — it is exactly what KeyInvest's risk culture is built to support.

Section 2 of 4 — The Three Lines of Defence
2
Who owns the risk?
KeyInvest uses the Three Lines of Defence (3LoD) model. It is the structure that tells us — for any risk, in any part of the business — who owns it, who reviews it, and who provides independent assurance.
1
Line 1 — Business Units. You. Day-to-day risk owners. You identify risk in your work, operate the controls, and report when something is wrong.
2
Line 2 — Risk & Compliance. Independent oversight. They set the framework, challenge Line 1, review risk profile, and report to the Board.
3
Line 3 — Internal Audit. Independent assurance to the Board that Lines 1 and 2 are working as intended.
Distribution team — Tuesday morning
A colleague says: "I don't really need to worry about risk — that's what Risk & Compliance is for. They'll let me know if I'm doing something wrong."
How would you respond?
Your responsibilities as Line 1
  • Identify risks in your own work — including new or emerging ones.
  • Operate the controls assigned to your role. Don't bypass them.
  • Report Incidents and raise Issues promptly — Risk & Compliance cannot manage what they do not see.
  • Cooperate with Root Cause Analysis when something has gone wrong.
  • Speak up when a process or culture is creating risk — even if no one has asked.

Section 3 of 4 — Incident or Issue?
3
Knowing the difference matters
Two of the most common risk terms at KeyInvest are Incident and Issue. They sound similar but mean different things — and they have different reporting obligations and timelines.
!
Incident. Something has happened — an unplanned event that disrupted, or could have disrupted, operations, information security, or compliance. Must be logged in Drova promptly and within required policy timeframes (typically within 24 hours of discovery).
i
Issue. A control is weak, missing, or not working — a gap that could cause a problem if left unaddressed. Must be raised in Drova promptly and within required policy timeframes (typically within 5 business days of identification).
If you are unsure whether something is an Incident or an Issue, raise it anyway — do not delay reporting while you decide.
Finance team — Thursday afternoon
You're reviewing a process and realise the supervisor approval step has never actually been switched on in the system — it has just been assumed for months. No payments have gone wrong (that anyone has noticed), but the control everyone thought existed does not exist.
Is this an Incident or an Issue?
A simple way to remember
  • Incident = something happened. There is an event with a date and time.
  • Issue = something could happen because a control is weak, missing, or broken.
  • An Incident often causes an Issue — when remediation is needed after the event. If an Incident is rated Medium or higher, raising an Issue in Drova to track the remediation is mandatory.
  • You don't need to make the final call yourself. Raise it. Risk & Compliance will help classify and rate it.
  • The worst thing you can do is nothing.

Section 4 of 4 — Your obligation to act
4
Scenario: when it's not your area
Any team member
Walking past a colleague's desk on Friday, you notice an unattended laptop unlocked, with a member's personal details open on screen and a printout of bank details sitting on top. The colleague is on the phone in another room. It is not your team and not your responsibility.
What should you do?
Every staff member has a role in risk management
1
Identify. Notice when something looks wrong — even if it's not your area.
2
Act. Take the immediate safe action — secure the data, stop the process, lock the screen.
3
Report. Log it in Drova. Tell your manager. Loop in Risk & Compliance. The Incident Management Policy applies to all staff and all activities.
This aligns directly with the intent of APRA CPS 220, which requires staff to identify and manage risk within their role. A Risk Management Framework only works if staff act on what they see. The framework remains documentation until staff apply it in practice.
